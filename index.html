A company scoring 80/100 is financially attractive by these criteria; a company at 45/100 has serious questions to answer.

Strategy Analysis
Porter's Five Forces

Before analysing a company's financials, you need to understand the industry it operates in — because the industry structure largely determines what margins are achievable and what costs are unavoidable. Porter's Five Forces is the standard framework for this. It maps five competitive pressures that, together, define how attractive an industry is to operate in. The higher the combined pressure from these forces, the harder it is for any company in that industry to earn strong, durable profits.

The key insight is that these forces have direct, measurable financial consequences. When you see a company with razor-thin margins, high SG&A, and rising accounts receivable, those numbers are often the financial translation of competitive forces at work — not just management decisions. Understanding the forces helps you explain the numbers, rather than just describing them.

1 — Competitive Rivalry
How intensely do existing firms compete? High rivalry means companies cut prices to win market share, or spend heavily on R&D and marketing to differentiate. Either way, profitability suffers. Key drivers: number of competitors, industry growth rate, switching costs, degree of product differentiation.
↓ Gross margins · ↑ SG&A · ↑ R&D
2 — Threat of New Entrants
How easily can new competitors enter the market? If barriers to entry are low, incumbents must constantly invest to protect their position — through brand spending, price reductions, or locking in distribution channels. High barriers (patents, regulation, scale, switching costs) protect margins long-term.
↑ Capex · ↑ Marketing · ↑ Intangibles
3 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers
Can suppliers raise input prices or impose unfavourable terms? Strong supplier power squeezes the company's cost base. It is high when there are few alternative suppliers, when the input is critical and hard to substitute, or when the supplier's product is a large share of the company's total costs.
↑ COGS · ↓ Gross margin · ↓ Net margin
4 — Bargaining Power of Buyers
Can customers demand lower prices, better quality, or more favourable payment terms? Buyer power is high when customers are few and large, when products are undifferentiated, or when switching costs are low. High buyer power compresses revenue per unit and can extend Days' Receivables as customers impose longer payment terms.
↓ Revenue/unit · ↑ DSO · ↓ Op. margin
5 — Threat of Substitutes
Can customers meet the same need with a different product or service? Substitutes constrain pricing power even without direct competition. The threat is high when a substitute offers a better price-performance ratio and when switching costs are low. It tends to cap revenue growth and erode asset utilisation over time.
↓ Volume · ↓ Revenue growth · ↑ R&D need
How to use it in practice
Rate each force as High / Medium / Low for the industry you are analysing. Then ask: which force most directly explains the financial structure you observe in the company's statements? A high gross margin despite intense rivalry suggests a strong competitive position. A low gross margin in a low-rivalry industry should make you suspicious — something structural is destroying value.
What the analysis can reveal — patterns to look for
Gross margin significantly above sector peers → the company has likely neutralised at least one force — either buyer power (pricing power), supplier power (sourcing advantage), or rivalry (differentiated product). Find out which one and whether it is durable.
SG&A growing faster than revenue → rivalry is intensifying or the threat of new entrants is rising. The company is spending more to defend the same position. If gross margin holds but net margin falls, the competitive battle is happening in the cost structure, not the price.
Rising Days' Receivables (DSO) over multiple years → strong signal of buyer power increasing. Customers are extracting longer payment terms. Also watch for revenue growing but cash from operations stagnating — a classic DSO trap.
Capex rising sharply relative to depreciation → could indicate the threat of new entrants forcing incumbents to upgrade infrastructure, or rivalry driving capacity investment. Ask whether the capex is offensive (growth) or defensive (protection).
Stable or improving margins in a high-rivalry sector → this is the most valuable signal in Porter analysis. It means the company has built a moat. Investigate the source: switching costs, network effects, cost advantage, or brand. That moat is the core of the investment thesis.
Real example — The global streaming industry illustrates all five forces at once. Netflix faces intense rivalry (Disney+, Prime Video, HBO Max), moderate barriers to entry for niche players, and extremely high buyer power since monthly subscriptions cost next to nothing to cancel. The financial consequence was unavoidable for years: Netflix spent ~$17 billion per year on content just to hold its subscriber base, keeping free cash flow structurally thin through 2022. From 2023 onward, password-sharing crackdowns and ad-supported tiers shifted the economics — but the underlying forces that made the first decade so capital-intensive remain a textbook illustration of how competitive pressure translates directly into financial structure.
Strategy Analysis
Cost Leadership vs Differentiation

Porter's Five Forces tells you about the industry. Competitive strategy tells you about the company's chosen position within that industry. Every company must choose how to compete — and that choice creates a predictable, readable financial fingerprint. Understanding which strategy a company is pursuing helps you interpret its cost structure, its margin profile, and whether its financial decisions make sense.

There are two fundamental options. A cost leader wins by being the cheapest viable option — it keeps costs lower than any competitor and competes primarily on price. A differentiator wins by offering something genuinely unique that customers are willing to pay a premium for — a brand, a product, a service, or an experience that competitors cannot easily replicate.

Cost Leadership
The goal is operational efficiency at scale. This means simplified product designs, standardised processes, lean supply chains, and minimal spending on anything that does not directly reduce unit costs. Marketing is kept low because the product sells itself on price.
What to expect in the financials:
→ Low to moderate gross margin
→ High asset turnover (revenue per £ of assets)
→ Low SG&A as % of revenue
→ Volume-driven revenue growth
Differentiation
The goal is uniqueness that justifies a price premium. This requires sustained investment in brand, R&D, customer experience, or product quality — the exact dimensions depend on the industry. The company must spend to remain differentiated, or the premium erodes.
What to expect in the financials:
→ High gross margin (pricing power)
→ High SG&A and/or R&D spend
→ Strong brand assets (intangibles)
→ Pricing-driven margin expansion
The trap to avoid
A company trying to be both — competitive on price and premium on quality simultaneously — typically ends up being neither. Porter called this being "stuck in the middle." In financial terms it shows up as mediocre margins in both directions: not lean enough to win on cost, not premium enough to command a price advantage.
What the analysis can reveal — reading the financial fingerprint
High gross margin + high SG&A as % of revenue → classic differentiator profile. The premium pricing is working (gross margin), but brand and marketing investment is required to maintain it. Ask whether gross margin is stable or improving — if SG&A is rising while gross margin erodes, the differentiation is weakening.
Low gross margin + very high asset turnover → cost leader profile. The company compensates thin unit margins with volume. The risk is that any volume disruption (recession, new entrant underpricing) immediately hits net margin hard. Check whether the cost advantage is structural (scale, proprietary process) or temporary.
Gross margin declining while SG&A holds constant → the differentiation is eroding. Either competitors have caught up, or the company is under pricing pressure it cannot resist. This is an early warning signal before it shows up in net margin.
High R&D spend sustained over years + rising gross margin → the R&D is producing durable competitive advantage. The company is a differentiator building a moat through innovation. Compare R&D as % of revenue to peers — leaders typically outspend, not match, the sector average.
Average gross margin + average SG&A + average net margin → the "stuck in the middle" signal. No clear competitive identity. These companies often underperform over long periods unless a strategic shift forces a choice.
Real example — Ryanair (cost leadership) strips every non-essential from the flying experience. No seat assignment, baggage fees on everything, minimal cabin service. Its cost per seat is the lowest in European aviation, which lets it undercut rivals on ticket prices while maintaining operating margins above 20%. Ferrari (differentiation) operates on the opposite logic entirely: it produces fewer than 15,000 cars per year deliberately, maintains a multi-year waiting list, and earns EBIT margins above 25% — because scarcity and brand are the product, not the car itself.
Balance Sheet Analysis
Common-Size & Trend Analysis

Raw financial numbers — assets of €4.3 billion, revenue of €8.1 billion — tell you very little on their own. To make numbers meaningful, you need to normalise them. Common-size and trend analysis are the two standard ways to do this, and together they give you a complete picture of both how a company has evolved over time and how it compares to peers.

Trend Analysis (Horizontal)

Pick a base year and set every line item to 100. In every subsequent year, each item is expressed as an index relative to that base. A value of 135 means that item has grown 35% since the base year. A value of 82 means it has shrunk by 18%.

Index = (Current Year Value / Base Year Value) × 100
What it reveals: the pace of change in each balance sheet item over time, and whether different parts of the business are growing at the same rate or diverging.
Critical rule: trend values can only be used within a single company. You cannot compare a trend index of 180 at Company A with a trend index of 140 at Company B — they use different base values.
Common-Size Analysis (Vertical)

Express every line item as a percentage of a single reference figure — total assets for the balance sheet, revenue for the income statement. This makes the internal proportions of the financial statement visible and comparable, regardless of the absolute size of the company.

% = (Line Item / Reference) × 100
Reference item: Total Assets (balance sheet) or Revenue / Net Sales (income statement).
Key advantage: common-size values CAN be compared across companies — this is what makes peer comparison possible. A company with inventory at 22% of total assets is more inventory-heavy than a peer at 8%, regardless of size.
What to look for when interpreting
On a trend analysis: are receivables growing faster than revenue? That suggests the company is struggling to collect payments. Is debt growing much faster than equity? That signals increasing financial leverage. On a common-size analysis: does a company have far more intangible assets as a % of total assets than its peers? It may have grown through acquisitions, carrying goodwill risk. Does it hold much more cash? It may be overly conservative — or preparing a large transaction.
What the analysis can reveal — patterns that matter
Receivables trend index rising faster than revenue trend index → the company is booking sales but not collecting them at the same pace. This can precede a write-down of bad debt, or it can signal that revenue is being pulled forward artificially. Always cross-check with the cash flow statement — does operating cash flow track reported profit, or diverge from it?
Inventory trend rising much faster than COGS → products are accumulating faster than they are being sold. Either demand is weakening (the company over-produced) or the company is building stock ahead of a price increase. Context from the sector and management commentary will disambiguate — but the financial flag is clear.
Common-size: COGS as % of revenue rising year on year → gross margin compression. Could be input cost inflation (supplier power increasing), competitive pricing pressure (rivalry), or product mix shifting toward lower-margin lines. This is the most important trend to track on the income statement common-size.
Common-size: debt as % of total assets rising while equity share falls → leverage increasing. Not automatically bad — if ROE is rising in parallel, the debt is being deployed productively. If ROE is flat or falling while leverage rises, the company is borrowing to stand still: a red flag for long-term solvency.
Trend: goodwill growing sharply as a % of total assets → the company is growing through acquisitions. Goodwill represents the premium paid above book value. If those acquisitions underperform, goodwill impairment charges can wipe out years of reported profit at once. A rising goodwill/total assets ratio is not a problem by itself, but it raises the question: is the company creating value through M&A, or just buying revenue growth?
Real example — Apple holds cash and equivalents equivalent to roughly 20% of its total assets. Expressed as a common-size figure, that 20% is directly comparable to Microsoft (~15%) or Alphabet (~25%) — you immediately see which tech giant sits on the largest cash cushion relative to its asset base, without being distorted by the fact that Apple's total assets are $350B while a smaller peer might be $30B. The common-size figure tells you the proportion; the trend index would tell you whether that proportion has been growing or shrinking over time.
Working Capital Analysis
Working Capital · WCN · Net Cash

A company's balance sheet can be read in two ways. The standard way lists assets on one side and liabilities on the other. The financial structure approach reorganises those same items into three linked balances that reveal something far more useful: how the company is funded at each level of its operations, and whether that funding structure is healthy, fragile, or exceptional.

The three balances are always connected by one equation that must hold: NC = WC − WCN. If it does not hold, there is an error. This relationship is the core of the Statement of Financial Structure.

Working Capital (WC) = Long-Term Capital − Fixed Assets
WC measures whether the long-term financing of the company (equity + long-term debt) is sufficient to cover its long-term investments (fixed assets). If WC is positive, there is a surplus of long-term capital available to fund the operating cycle. If WC is negative, the company is using short-term resources to finance long-term assets — a structurally fragile situation that increases financial risk.
Working Capital Need (WCN) = Current Assets (excl. cash) − Current Liabilities (excl. overdraft)
WCN measures the net funding requirement of the operating cycle — the day-to-day business of buying, producing, and selling. If WCN is positive, the company needs cash to bridge the gap between paying suppliers and collecting from customers. If WCN is negative (a working capital resource), customers pay before the company pays its suppliers — the operating cycle generates cash rather than consuming it. This is the case for most pure retailers and online businesses.
Net Cash (NC) = Positive Cash − Bank Overdraft = WC − WCN
NC is the balancing item — the excess (or deficit) of working capital after meeting the working capital need. Positive NC means the company has surplus cash; negative NC means it is relying on bank overdrafts. NC also tells you whether the company can fund growth: if WCN is likely to increase as revenue grows, does the company have enough NC to absorb that increase?
The six financial structures — what combination means what
Case 1: WC+ · WCN+ · NC+
Standard manufacturing company. WC covers WCN with cash left over. Healthy.
Case 2: WC+ · WCN+ · NC−
WC not enough to cover WCN. Relying on overdraft. Needs monitoring.
Case 3: WC+ · WCN− · NC+
Retailer / online model. Operating cycle generates cash. Excellent structure.
Case 4: WC− · WCN− · NC+
WCN resource more than offsets negative WC. Unusual — watch closely.
Case 5: WC− · WCN− · NC−
Negative WC, overdraft covering the gap. Risky — LT assets funded short-term.
Case 6: WC− · WCN+ · NC−
Most fragile. Overdraft funding both LT assets and the operating cycle. Very risky.
Real example — McDonald's runs a structurally negative WCN: franchise operators pay royalties and fees upfront, while McDonald's pays its own suppliers on extended credit terms. Customers pay at the counter instantly. The result is that McDonald's operating cycle generates cash rather than consuming it — a working capital resource. This is why the company can carry substantial long-term debt and still return billions to shareholders through buybacks and dividends each year, without straining its liquidity.
What the analysis can reveal — questions to ask
WCN rising in line with revenue growth → normal and expected. The operating cycle simply scales with the business. No alarm, but worth quantifying: how much additional WCN does €1 of new revenue require? If it's high, fast growth will consume cash quickly and may require external financing.
WCN rising faster than revenue → the operating cycle is becoming less efficient. Either receivables are growing (collecting more slowly), inventory is building up (selling more slowly), or payables are falling (paying suppliers faster, losing leverage). Identify which component is driving it using the CCC.
WC positive but NC turning negative → the company is starting to rely on bank overdrafts to fund its operating cycle. WC is sufficient in theory, but the WCN has grown beyond it. This is Case 2 — it needs monitoring. If NC continues to worsen while revenue grows, the company may need to raise equity or refinance debt.
Sudden shift from WCN+ to WCN− → unusual and worth investigating carefully. It can be positive (the business model has shifted toward faster cash collection — e.g. launching a subscription model) or negative (suppliers are refusing to extend credit, forcing the company to pay faster). Context is everything here.
Negative WC (Case 5 or 6) → long-term assets are being funded by short-term resources. This is structurally fragile: if short-term financing dries up (credit lines pulled, commercial paper market freezes), the company faces an acute liquidity crisis even if it is profitable. This is how solvent companies can collapse quickly.
Working Capital Analysis
Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

The WCN tells you whether the operating cycle is a need or a resource at a point in time. The Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) tells you the same thing but in days — how many days it takes for a company to convert its investments in inventory and receivables back into cash. It is the operational, dynamic counterpart to the static WCN measure, and it is directly comparable across companies and sectors.

The CCC is built from three components, each measuring a different phase of the operating cycle. Understanding each one separately before combining them is essential to diagnosing where a company's cash is being tied up — or freed.

DSO — Days' Receivables
= 360 × Avg. A/R / Revenue
How many days on average does it take to collect payment after a sale? A DSO of 45 days means the company typically waits 45 days after delivering a product or service before the cash lands in its account. Lower is better — it means customers pay quickly. A rising DSO over time may indicate that the company is struggling to collect, or that it is extending credit to win sales it would not otherwise make.
DIO — Days' Inventories
= 360 × Avg. Inv. / Cost of Sales
How many days does inventory sit on the shelf before being sold? A DIO of 60 means goods spend two months in stock before moving. Lower is generally better — it means faster turnover, fresher stock, and less cash tied up in warehouses. Very high DIO may signal overstocking, slow-moving products, or demand weakness. However, some industries naturally carry high DIO (wine, aerospace) because the business model requires it.
DPO — Days' Payables
= 360 × Avg. A/P / Cost of Sales
How many days does the company take to pay its own suppliers? Here, higher is better — a long DPO means the company is essentially borrowing from its suppliers interest-free. It reflects bargaining power: large, dominant buyers can impose 90-day payment terms on smaller suppliers. A very short DPO may mean weak negotiating leverage, or a deliberate choice to pay early for discounts.
CCC = DSO + DIO − DPO
The CCC combines all three. A positive CCC (e.g. 40 days) means the company needs to fund 40 days of operations from its own cash or credit lines — cash is tied up. A negative CCC means the company collects from customers before it pays suppliers — it is being financed by the supply chain, not the other way around. Negative CCC is a structural advantage that allows companies to scale without proportional capital requirements.
What good vs bad looks like — by sector
Retail and consumer goods: CCC between −30 and +30 days is normal. A negative CCC is a strong structural advantage. Manufacturing and industrials: CCC between 30 and 90 days is typical — inventory cycles are long. Pharmaceuticals and aerospace: CCC can exceed 100 days, because development cycles and production lead times are inherently long. Software and services: DSO dominates (no inventory), and a CCC of 20–40 days is standard. Context always matters — compare within the same sector.
What the analysis can reveal — reading changes over time
DSO rising year on year → customers are taking longer to pay. Before concluding this is negative, check: is the company entering new markets where credit terms are different (e.g. B2B vs B2C)? Is it deliberately extending credit to drive growth? Or is it simply struggling to collect? Cross-reference with the bad debt provision on the balance sheet — if it's rising too, the receivables quality is deteriorating.
DIO rising while revenue is flat or falling → the most dangerous inventory signal. The company produced for demand that didn't materialise. Excess inventory ties up cash, risks obsolescence, and will eventually require markdowns that destroy gross margin. Watch for sudden inventory write-downs in the notes to the accounts.
DPO falling significantly → the company is losing bargaining power with suppliers, or switching to early-payment discount programmes. Both are worth understanding. If rivals have stable or rising DPO while this company's falls, the competitive position is weakening on the supply side.
CCC worsening (rising) while revenue grows strongly → a classic growth trap. The business is scaling, but the working capital need is growing faster than earnings. Without careful cash management or access to credit, a fast-growing company can run out of cash even while posting strong profit growth. This is sometimes called overtrading.
CCC improving consistently → operational execution is getting better. The company is collecting faster, turning inventory faster, or negotiating better supplier terms. Often overlooked, a steadily improving CCC is one of the most reliable indicators of management quality — it shows discipline in the mechanics of the business, not just the headline numbers.
Real example — Inditex (Zara's parent) is one of the most studied examples of CCC efficiency in retail. Its design-to-shelf cycle is under three weeks. It holds minimal inventory, collects cash at point of sale, and pays suppliers on 60-day terms. The result is a near-zero or negative CCC — the business effectively funds its own growth from operations. When Inditex opens new stores or expands into new markets, the additional working capital need is minimal, which is a direct financial advantage over slower fashion retailers who sit on months of stock.
Balance Sheet Analysis
Liquidity & Solvency Ratios

Profitability tells you whether a company makes money. Liquidity and solvency tell you whether it can survive. A company can be profitable on paper and still go bankrupt — if it cannot meet its short-term obligations as they fall due, or if its debt burden becomes unserviceable. These ratios are the first things a credit analyst or lender looks at, and they should be part of any serious equity analysis too.

There is an important distinction between the two categories. Liquidity ratios measure short-term survival — can the company pay what is due in the next 12 months? Solvency ratios measure long-term structural stability — is the overall debt load manageable relative to earnings and equity?

Liquidity Ratios
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
Measures whether the company has enough short-term assets to cover its short-term obligations. A ratio above 1.5x is generally considered comfortable. Below 1.0x means current liabilities exceed current assets — a potential red flag. However, context matters: retailers with very short inventory cycles and fast cash collection can operate safely below 1.0x.
Healthy: >1.5x · Caution: 1.0–1.5x · Risk: <1.0x
Quick Ratio = (Current Assets − Inventory) / Current Liabilities
A stricter version of the current ratio that removes inventory — because inventory takes time to sell and may not be easily convertible to cash in a crisis. It asks: if business stopped tomorrow, could we pay our short-term creditors from cash and receivables alone? Also called the "acid test."
Healthy: >1.0x · Concern for inventory-heavy firms if <0.5x
Solvency Ratios
Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity
Measures how much of the company is financed by debt versus equity. A high ratio means the company is heavily leveraged — higher return potential, but also higher risk since debt must be repaid regardless of performance. Thresholds vary enormously by sector: utilities and banks routinely operate above 2x, while tech companies often run close to zero.
Varies by sector — always compare within the same industry
Interest Coverage = EBIT / Interest Expense
Measures how many times over a company can pay its interest charges from operating profit. A ratio of 5x means EBIT covers interest five times — comfortable. Below 3x warrants attention: the company has limited buffer if earnings deteriorate. Below 1.5x is a danger signal — a modest drop in operating performance could make interest payments unaffordable.
Strong: >5x · Adequate: 3–5x · Caution: 1.5–3x · Stress: <1.5x
Net Debt / EBITDA
How many years of EBITDA would it take to repay all net debt? This is the most widely used leverage metric in professional credit and M&A analysis. Below 1x is conservative. 2–3x is standard for investment-grade companies. Above 4–5x raises questions about debt sustainability.
Conservative: <1x · Standard: 1–3x · Elevated: >4x
The most important thing to remember
No ratio should be read in isolation. A current ratio of 0.8x is alarming for a manufacturer but normal for a supermarket chain. A debt-to-equity of 3x is routine for a regulated utility but dangerous for a cyclical industrial. Always ask: is this ratio consistent with the company's business model, sector norms, and the stage of its development? Then ask whether it has improved or deteriorated over the past three years — trend matters as much as level.
What the analysis can reveal — combinations that signal risk or strength
Current ratio falling below 1.0x + quick ratio well below 0.5x → acute short-term liquidity stress. The company cannot cover near-term obligations from liquid assets. Before alarming, check the credit facility disclosures: does the company have undrawn revolving credit lines? If not, this warrants serious attention — especially if operating cash flow is also negative.
High current ratio (e.g. 3x+) in a manufacturing company → not automatically positive. It may mean excess inventory or slow-moving receivables are inflating current assets. A high current ratio driven by cash is healthy; one driven by inventory or doubtful receivables is misleading. Always decompose what is inside current assets.
Net Debt/EBITDA rising while interest coverage falls → the company is taking on more debt while its ability to service it deteriorates. This is the most dangerous double signal in solvency analysis. If both metrics cross their warning thresholds simultaneously, the risk of a credit rating downgrade — or covenant breach — increases sharply.
Debt/Equity rising but interest coverage stable or improving → the company is using leverage productively. EBIT is growing faster than interest expense. This can be a sign of deliberate, disciplined financial engineering — common in mature companies with predictable cash flows (infrastructure, consumer staples).
Net Debt/EBITDA negative (i.e. net cash position) → the company holds more cash than gross debt. This is conservatively positioned and low risk from a solvency standpoint — but also raises a question: is the company being too cautious with its capital? Excess cash that earns nothing while the business could be investing or returning capital to shareholders is a form of value destruction.
Interest coverage below 1.5x for two consecutive years → the company is living on borrowed time with its creditors. Debt covenants — which typically require coverage above 2x or 2.5x — may already be at risk of being breached. A covenant breach forces renegotiation, often at punitive terms, and can trigger acceleration clauses that make all debt immediately due.
Real example — During the 2020 Covid crisis, airlines revealed a fundamental liquidity problem: they had sold tickets (current liabilities: deferred revenue) without yet flying the passengers, while simultaneously unable to fly due to lockdowns. Their current ratios collapsed, interest coverage fell below 1x at many carriers, and Net Debt/EBITDA became meaningless because EBITDA turned negative. The companies that survived were those with the strongest pre-crisis balance sheets — Ryanair's conservative liquidity management gave it the buffer to weather the disruption and emerge in a stronger competitive position than weaker peers.

Why this project exists

Most students enter the finance industry having studied financial analysis in theory but having never actually done it on a real company, with real stakes, in public. The gap between academic knowledge and applied skill is enormous — and the standard way to close it is to wait until you are hired and learn on the job.

Fundamental Edge is an attempt to close that gap before entering the industry. The premise is simple: the best way to develop a skill is to practice it consistently, at a high standard, and with external feedback from people who know what good looks like. So that is what this project is — a structured, public, peer-reviewed practice programme in equity research, running continuously throughout four years of study.

Every analysis is independent. There are no financial relationships with any company covered. Every verdict — buy, avoid, neutral — is the result of the methodology applied to the data, nothing else. Every piece of professional feedback received is published openly, including the parts that correct mistakes or challenge conclusions. The goal is not to appear knowledgeable. It is to actually become good at this.
